Yearly Commemoration

'The poppies, row on row'

"The poppies blow
between the crosses
row on row"

   Every 11 November, the whole of Britain fills up with poppies. Many proudly decorate themselves with these flowers to commemorate the First World War and those who had fallen then. In this article, we explore the origin and extent of this culture.

   Some members of the British commonwealth (mostly Australia, Canada and New Zealand) annually commemorate the ending of the First World War on the 11th hour of the 11th day of the 11th month in 1918.

Why Poppies?


"A watch can bring
out the best in us"

   The connotation with poppies has to do with a certain poem that was written during WWI by an allied soldier. As the poem iterates ‘We are the Dead’, it is mostly used to show solidarity with the fallen in a poetic if not liminal (the no man’s land between life and death) way.

   This poem is called ‘In Flanders Fields’, was written by Canadian medic John McCrae and goes as such:

In Flanders fields the poppies blow
Between the crosses, row on row,
That mark our place; and in the sky
The larks, still bravely singing, fly
Scarce heard amid the guns below.

We are the Dead. Short days ago
We lived, felt dawn, saw sunset glow,
Loved and were loved, and now we lie
In Flanders fields.

Take up our quarrel with the foe:
To you from failing hands we throw
The torch; be yours to hold it high.
If ye break faith with us who die
We shall not sleep, though poppies grow
In Flanders fields.

Context

   John McRae wrote this as he was mourning the death of his fellow soldier. His friend Lieutenant Helmer had died in the dreadful Second battle of Ypres, which was a large-scale assault with sizeable losses on Britain’s side.

   The Belgian city of Ypres was one of the most deadly places of WWI in general. Besides its beauty and liminal character, In Flanders Fields partly became so popular because it was written in a battlefield where many other soldiers had died. It was so relatable, that it received immediate popularity and now sits at the forefront of the commemoration day. The central character of the poppy meant that it was used as national symbol to commemorate the fallen of WWI, which is called The Great War in Great Britain.
